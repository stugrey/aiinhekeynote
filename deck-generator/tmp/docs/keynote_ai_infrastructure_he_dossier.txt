1. Executive synthesis
The conference programme is rich in pedagogy, assessment, ethics, literacy, and practice. Your keynote can do something different and complementary: make the infrastructure question explicit. The strongest version of the talk is not anti-AI and not anti-industry. It is anti-passivity. It argues that universities are moving from occasional tool use to structural dependence, and that once AI is infrastructural the key question shifts from capability to control, accountability, resilience, and bargaining power.
The central strategic move is to reframe adoption as governance. Instead of asking only whether a model is impressive, cheap, or convenient, the talk asks who shapes the defaults built into teaching, assessment, advising, analytics, and institutional decision-making. That reframe allows you to connect seemingly separate topics in the programme—assessment redesign, learner analytics, AI literacy, ethics, accessibility, vendor platforms, and institutional strategy—into one coherent argument.
The talk works best as a three-step argument. First, AI is already becoming invisible infrastructure in higher education: student use is mainstream, assessment has already changed, and tools are being stitched into everyday workflows. Second, the infrastructure layer is highly concentrated, capital-intensive, and increasingly controlled by external firms whose incentives are not identical to the public mission of universities. Third, lock-in is happening now, while standards, contracts, norms, integrations, and expectations are still fluid. This is the small window in which universities can still move from passive consumption to active stewardship.
What the room most needs from this keynote Your keynote should sound like a governance keynote for a room that has mostly been invited to think about pedagogy, productivity, and assessment. That difference is your advantage. It lets you connect the whole programme rather than compete with the sessions already focused on tools.
Three claims worth making clearly and early
• AI in higher education is no longer just a set of optional tools. It is becoming part of the substrate through which teaching, assessment, support, and governance are delivered.
• Infrastructure matters because it shapes what institutions can see, do, audit, export, contest, and change. Control does not have to mean owning every component, but it does require legibility, bargaining power, portability, and evaluation capacity.
• The practical choice is not between total in-house sovereignty and total outsourcing. It is between institutions that remain capable stewards of their AI stack and institutions that become captive customers inside an extractive ecosystem.
The overall tone to strike
• Serious, practical, and public-interest oriented rather than apocalyptic.
• Critical of structural dependency, but not dismissive of vendors, open-source communities, or the genuine usefulness of AI systems.
• Comfortable with experimentation, but insistent that experimentation without governance becomes lock-in by default.
• Focused on educational purpose: what universities are for, what students deserve, and what academic values require from infrastructure.
One-sentence takeaway
Universities are not only deciding whether to use AI; they are helping fix the defaults that may govern higher education for years, and the question is whether they will do so as passive customers or active stewards.

What the abstract quietly gets right
• It treats AI as infrastructure rather than as a novelty. That is more analytically useful and more future-proof.
• It avoids the false choice between naive enthusiasm and total rejection.
• It identifies the correct policy levers. Procurement and evaluation are often missing from higher education discussions that stay at the level of pedagogy or ethics.
• It foregrounds accountability to academic values and student interests, which stops the talk from drifting into abstract sovereignty rhetoric.
What to sharpen in the live talk
• Define invisible infrastructure fast and concretely. Do not assume the room will instantly hear the depth of the claim.

• Use one or two examples of fragility that are educationally legible before moving to broader geopolitical or theoretical cases.
• Name the third position explicitly: neither passive consumerism nor romantic self-sufficiency, but active stewardship.
• End with institutional decisions, not just concerns.

4. Annotated concept notebook: every note, definition, and linked idea expanded
This section expands every topic in your note list and turns it into usable keynote material. For each item, the aim is the same: define the concept, explain why it matters for this talk, show how to use it, and flag any caveat or overclaim to avoid.
How to use this section A good keynote does not need to mention all of these concepts explicitly. The value here is optionality. You can choose two or three main frameworks for the spoken talk and keep the rest as backup material for transitions, questions, or later writing.
The Benefits of Bubbles: when speculative booms build durable infrastructure
Definition: Ben Thompson's argument is that some bubbles are socially productive because overinvestment can finance infrastructure and ecosystem formation before ordinary market logic would. The bubble may still be a bubble, but it can leave behind assets that become genuinely useful.
Deep dive: For your keynote, this is valuable because it stops the analysis from collapsing into 'AI hype is fake'. The stronger claim is that AI may well be in an overinvestment phase and still be building real compute capacity, software stacks, integration layers, and organisational habits that higher education will end up using. The question then becomes not whether the boom is entirely rational, but who owns the infrastructure after the exuberance settles and under what conditions universities gain access to it.
How it helps the keynote: Use this idea to frame the AI boom as politically consequential rather than merely speculative. It lets you say: even if the bubble produces socially useful infrastructure, universities should not confuse access with control. The public value question begins after the data centres are built.
Caution: Do not imply that all AI spending is wise or that every bubble is productive. Your contrast is between bubbles that leave behind infrastructure and bubbles that leave behind almost nothing of public value.
Possible line: The question is not whether the AI boom will build useful infrastructure. It probably will. The question is who gets to govern that infrastructure when universities start to treat it as normal.

Bad bubbles: tulips, Beanie Babies, Pogs, Panini stickers, bitcoin as rhetorical contrasts
Definition: These examples function as shorthand for speculative manias in which asset prices detach from durable underlying value, or at least from the kind of shared infrastructure value that would justify broad public dependence.
Deep dive: They matter because they help you distinguish between two kinds of hype story. One is simple speculative frenzy. The other is an investment surge that can also accelerate real build-out. AI looks more like the second category than the first because it is tied to datacentres, chips, networking, developer tooling, and enterprise integration, even if many valuations and claims may still be inflated.
How it helps the keynote: Use these examples briefly and rhetorically, not as the centre of the analysis. They are useful for a sentence or a slide contrast: not every bubble leaves behind railways, fibre, or compute; some only leave behind collectibles and regret.
Caution: Do not spend long on financial analogies. The point is simply to create conceptual contrast before moving back to higher education.

ICC account disruption and the digital-sovereignty lesson
Definition: This note points to the widely discussed 2025 controversy around the International Criminal Court and Microsoft services. Reporting and later parliamentary answers make clear that the exact mechanics were disputed, so the safest framing is not 'Microsoft simply switched off the ICC', but that the episode exposed anxiety about dependence on cloud and software infrastructures that can be affected by sanctions, legal pressure, and geopolitical conflict.
Deep dive: The deep importance of the example is that infrastructure risk is not only about outages or price rises. It is also about jurisdiction, coercion, continuity, and the ability of institutions to keep operating when political pressures hit the layers they depend on. Microsoft's later European cloud commitments and resilience promises show that the episode was serious enough to trigger public reassurance and policy debate.
How it helps the keynote: Use this example later in the keynote, after simpler educational examples such as a learning-platform outage. It expands the argument from operational fragility to sovereignty and control. It says: dependence on external infrastructure is never only a technical matter.
Caution: Be precise. Say that the case raised digital-sovereignty concerns and that details of the account interruption were contested. Avoid oversimplified claims that could be rebutted easily.

Lessig's four modalities of regulation: law, norms, market, and code
Definition: Lawrence Lessig's key insight is that behaviour in digital environments is regulated by at least four interacting forces: law, social norms, markets, and code or architecture. Code matters because it determines what is possible, easy, difficult, visible, or impossible. Architecture is therefore not neutral background; it is a regulator.
Deep dive: This framework is particularly useful for higher-education AI because universities often talk as if policy alone will solve governance. Lessig helps show why policy documents are only one modality. Law may set obligations, but market concentration limits choices, local norms affect what staff and students actually do, and code determines defaults such as data retention, logging, explainability, exportability, and access control.
How it helps the keynote: Use Lessig as the bridge between abstract ethics and concrete system design. It lets you say that procurement is governance because procurement shapes code; code then shapes behaviour.
Caution: Keep the framework practical. The room does not need a theory lecture; it needs to see why institutional behaviour is shaped by architecture.

Viable System Model: how an institution can remain governable while adapting
Definition: Stafford Beer's viable system model describes how complex organisations stay coherent in changing environments. In shorthand: System 1 handles operations; System 2 coordinates; System 3 controls and allocates resources; System 4 scans the environment and adapts; System 5 defines policy, identity, and overall direction. The model is recursive: viable subsystems also need these functions.
Deep dive: This is a powerful governance lens for universities because AI adoption is currently often heavy on local experimentation and light on systemic coordination. Faculties, departments, and services may already be acting as System 1 units, experimenting with tools and workflows. But many institutions have weak System 2 coordination, patchy System 3 assurance, thin System 4 foresight and evaluation capability, and underdeveloped System 5 clarity about institutional purpose in an AI-mediated environment.
How it helps the keynote: Use the model to diagnose the current organisational pattern: lots of innovation at the edge, not enough capability at the centre to coordinate, evaluate, bargain, and set mission-level direction. It gives you a governance model that is richer than simply saying 'we need a policy'.
Caution: Keep the language plain. You can mention the model by name if the room is receptive, but the main payoff is the diagnosis.

The Times Higher Education argument: the real risk is irrelevance through non-engagement
Definition: The Times Higher Education opinion piece you flagged argues that universities risk irrelevance if they fail to engage fully with AI. Its underlying claim is that the biggest danger is not AI in the abstract but institutional inertia, lack of imagination, and treating AI only as a plagiarism or IT issue.
Deep dive: This is useful because it provides the most credible pro-engagement counterpart to your argument. You do not need to reject it. In fact, your keynote becomes stronger if it agrees that disengagement is a mistake and then adds the missing condition: engagement without stewardship simply hands institutional leverage to external actors.
How it helps the keynote: Use this source to signal that you are not defending a nostalgic return to pre-AI higher education. You are defending a more serious form of engagement—one that includes governance, bargaining power, and public-purpose design.
Possible line: The choice is not between engagement and resistance. The choice is between shallow engagement on someone else's terms and serious engagement on terms universities can help shape.

Dan McQuillan as the counter-pole: a critique of inevitability, extractivism, and harm
Definition: McQuillan's writing offers a sharper anti-GenAI position, linking generative AI to ecological cost, labour exploitation, austerity politics, concentration of power, and the erosion of social relations. It resists the framing that generative AI is simply inevitable progress to be integrated as quickly as possible.
Deep dive: This is valuable because it reminds you not to naturalise the current AI trajectory. It highlights the material and political costs that can disappear in managerial accounts of adoption. It also helps prevent the keynote from sounding like a governance-only talk that quietly accepts all the premises of Big Tech.
How it helps the keynote: Use McQuillan as a boundary marker, not as your main destination. You can borrow his insistence that alternatives remain possible and that the issue is not just classroom convenience. But your own position is more constructive and institution-building: universities should shape, constrain, co-design, and selectively build, not simply refuse.
Caution: Because your note says 'too far', treat McQuillan as an external critical pressure that sharpens your analysis without dictating your prescription.

Augment, Adopt, Align, Adapt: a practical behavioural framework for organisational AI use
Definition: The Behavioural Insights Team framework breaks the issue into four linked questions. Augment asks how AI can extend human capability rather than merely automate tasks. Adopt examines the conditions under which people will actually use systems, especially motivation, capability, and trust. Align asks whether systems reflect human intentions and values. Adapt asks how humans and institutions change in response to using AI over time.
Deep dive: This framework is unusually useful for your keynote because it translates abstract governance questions into behavioural and organisational design. It helps you say that good AI policy is not only about access or prohibition. It is about designing institutions that can use systems well, distrust them appropriately, evaluate them continuously, and adapt without surrendering educational purpose.
How it helps the keynote: You can use the four-part structure either as a hidden organising device for your policy section or explicitly as a slide. Motivation, capability, and trust are especially valuable because they explain why higher education adoption is messy: people use tools not only because they are available, but because they feel pressured, under-supported, or uncertain.

Path dependence, QWERTY, and the danger of early lock-in
Definition: Path dependence means that early choices, accidents, complementarities, and network effects can shape later outcomes in self-reinforcing ways. Once standards, habits, contracts, data formats, and training investments accumulate around a particular path, change becomes costly even when alternatives improve.
Deep dive: The classic QWERTY keyboard story is useful as a metaphor, but it should be handled carefully because the strongest historical claim—that QWERTY is obviously inferior and won by accident alone—has been challenged. The broader lesson remains sound: history matters, default choices matter, and coordination effects can entrench a system long after the original reasons for choosing it have faded.
How it helps the keynote: This is one of the best concepts for your keynote because AI in higher education is still in a fluid phase. Assessment practices, approved tools, integrations, data arrangements, vendor contracts, and user habits are settling now. That is exactly the moment in which path dependence takes shape.
Caution: Say that the QWERTY anecdote is a metaphor for lock-in rather than a simple proof that 'worse technologies always win'.

Creative work as a conversation with the medium
Definition: The idea here is that creative and intellectual work is not simply the execution of a pre-existing idea. It is an iterative conversation with the medium itself: writing changes thinking, sketching changes seeing, coding changes design, and drafting changes judgment. The medium pushes back.
Deep dive: You could not verify a single canonical source for this exact phrasing, so it is best treated as an unattributed conceptual principle rather than a formal quotation. Its importance for the keynote is substantial because it clarifies why higher education should not treat AI only as an answer engine. If students and staff increasingly think, write, and design through AI systems, the medium will shape their habits of inquiry, revision, confidence, and judgment.
How it helps the keynote: Use this idea to connect infrastructure to pedagogy. If the medium changes, the educational process changes. That means the infrastructure question is not external to teaching and learning; it sits inside them.
Caution: Do not present the phrase as a verified quote from a specific thinker unless you later source it securely.

Surveillance capitalism: from industrial extraction to behavioural extraction
Definition: Shoshana Zuboff's concept of surveillance capitalism names a regime in which human experience is rendered as data, captured as behavioural surplus, and used to generate prediction products and behavioural influence. The key move is not only extracting value from labour or manufacturing, but from the monitoring and modulation of behaviour itself.
Deep dive: For higher education, the relevance is that AI systems can turn interaction data into a valuable asset. Every prompt, click, revision, chat exchange, and feedback loop can become part of an optimisation apparatus. The risk is not only privacy loss in a narrow sense; it is that educational activity becomes legible mainly through the metrics and incentives of external platforms.
How it helps the keynote: Use surveillance capitalism to widen the argument beyond plagiarism or bias. Ask what happens when the behavioural traces of learning become raw material for systems optimised around engagement, retention, sales, or model improvement rather than around educational purpose.

Extractivism, data colonialism, and knowledge extractivism
Definition: These related ideas help differentiate levels of the problem. Data colonialism, associated with Nick Couldry and Ulises Mejias, describes the appropriation of everyday life in the form of data through unequal relations of power. Extractivism more broadly names the logic of taking value from people, environments, or communities without reciprocal stewardship. Knowledge extractivism, as in Matteo Pasquinelli's work, points to how AI systems extract patterns, labour, and intelligence from human traces and repackage them as proprietary or controlled computational assets.
Deep dive: This matters because higher education is a knowledge-producing environment. Teaching materials, student work, scholarly communication, advising interactions, and institutional records are not neutral raw materials waiting to be harvested. They are socially produced, mission-governed, and often rights-bearing. Once they are folded into external AI pipelines without clear terms, universities risk participating in the extraction of their own intellectual and relational commons.
How it helps the keynote: Use these concepts to sharpen the public-interest stakes. The issue is not merely that universities might pay too much for tools. It is that educational activity can be transformed into a source of value capture for actors whose accountability to students and academic communities is partial at best.

Most frontier models are built by industry, not by public-interest institutions
Definition: The empirical point here is straightforward: the overwhelming majority of notable frontier models now come from industry, and the frontier stack is financed by corporations with enormous capital budgets and clear profit motives. That does not mean public-interest actors are powerless. It means they should not mistake access to a model API for control over the strategic direction of the ecosystem.
Deep dive: This is the basis for a crucial practical argument in your keynote. Universities should not try to win a brute-force scaling race against hyperscalers. Their leverage lies elsewhere: domain data, educational workflows, trust relationships, institutional knowledge, and the ability to evaluate whether systems actually work for teaching, assessment, and support.
How it helps the keynote: Use this point to pivot to the build-versus-buy continuum. Universities may sometimes buy, sometimes partner, sometimes self-host smaller open models, sometimes add retrieval layers, and sometimes fine-tune. What they need in every case is evaluation capacity and governance clarity.

Language-centric scaling versus grounding critiques
Definition: The language-centric scaling view holds that because so much human knowledge is expressed in language, training on vast textual corpora can recover a surprising amount of world structure and practical competence. The grounding critique argues that language alone is not enough: human understanding is also sensorimotor, situational, embodied, institutional, and action-linked. Text-only fluency does not guarantee grounded understanding.
Deep dive: For your keynote, this distinction matters because universities can be seduced by generic linguistic competence. A model that sounds informed is not the same as a system that is grounded in local institutional rules, student needs, disciplinary standards, or educational outcomes. Grounding can come through retrieval, structured data, domain adaptation, workflow integration, and careful evaluation, but it must be designed.
How it helps the keynote: Use this concept to counter a simplistic scaling narrative. If universities want systems that genuinely work in their contexts, the answer is not always 'use a bigger general model'. Often it is 'use a more grounded, better-evaluated, more accountable one'.
Caution: Treat 'language hypothesis' and 'grounding hypothesis' as useful labels for a debate, not as rigid textbook categories.

Task versus purpose: the radiology lesson
Definition: A recurring mistake in automation discourse is to jump from task substitution to occupational or institutional disappearance. Radiology is a useful analogue because it has long been discussed as a domain ripe for AI automation, yet workforce demand and professional need have remained strong. The more realistic pattern is task reconfiguration: throughput, triage, prioritisation, checking, documentation, and supervision shift, but professional purpose persists and often becomes more visible.
Deep dive: For higher education, the question is therefore not only which tasks AI can perform, but what teaching, advising, assessment, and scholarly work are for. If the purpose includes judgment, trust, challenge, care, accountability, interpretation, and community formation, then AI changes the configuration of work rather than making the institution unnecessary.
How it helps the keynote: Use this argument to resist lazy substitution claims. It helps you align with the conference's pedagogic concerns while keeping the focus on infrastructure and purpose.
Possible line: The right question is not 'which tasks can AI do?' but 'what purposes must universities continue to serve, and how should tasks be reorganised in support of those purposes?'

Veritasium and the difference between information access and learning
Definition: The broad educational lesson associated with the Veritasium reference is that knowing the answer is not the same as learning. Durable learning depends on effort, retrieval, correction, feedback, accountability, and often productive struggle. A world in which answers are instantly available does not make teaching obsolete; it changes the role of teaching.
Deep dive: This is a useful companion to the programme's concern with designing learning for a world where answers are free. If answers are abundant, then infrastructure choices matter even more because they shape how students practise, draft, revise, verify, and receive feedback. AI systems can support or short-circuit learning depending on how they are integrated.
How it helps the keynote: Use this to keep the keynote grounded in educational purpose. The infrastructure question is not detached from learning theory; it affects whether systems cultivate dependence, confidence, or deep engagement.

Use institutional higher-education data and workflow knowledge rather than chasing pure scale
Definition: This note captures a very strong strategic insight. Universities are unlikely to outspend hyperscalers on frontier pretraining, but they do possess something more relevant to many real educational problems: domain-specific data, workflow knowledge, task definitions, quality criteria, and evaluative contexts. In many cases the winning move is not super-scaling a general model but grounding, adapting, or fine-tuning a smaller one for specific institutional purposes.
Deep dive: The practical implication is not 'dump all student data into a model'. It is the opposite: be selective, governed, and purpose-limited. Use de-identified, rights-cleared, or teacher-generated corpora where possible. Build retrieval and evaluation layers. Distinguish between operational use, model improvement, and external training. Treat student work and interaction traces as governed educational assets, not free raw material.
How it helps the keynote: Use this idea in the constructive half of the talk. It lets you propose a realistic stewardship agenda: local or sectoral eval suites, retrieval-grounded systems, curated fine-tuning data, and open-model experimentation where justified.
Caution: Because this area touches student rights, privacy, and copyright, the keynote should pair the opportunity claim with a clear governance claim.

5. Evidence base for the keynote
This section gives you the factual backbone for the talk. It is organised around the four claims most worth evidencing: AI use is already mainstream in higher education; institutional governance is lagging; the underlying infrastructure is concentrated and expensive; and meaningful alternatives exist through evaluation, grounding, and selective local or sectoral capability.
5.1 Adoption is ahead of governance
The headline educational fact is that student use is already mainstream. The 2026 HEPI student survey reports that 95 percent of UK full-time undergraduates use AI in at least one way and 94 percent use generative AI to help with assessed work. The same survey reports that 65 percent think assessment has changed significantly in response to AI. The 2025 HEPI survey already showed similar momentum one year earlier, along with limited training and mixed institutional encouragement.
The governance fact is that policy and organisational maturity are not keeping pace. Jisc's AI maturity work suggests most tertiary institutions are still in an experimental phase, and its governance guidance warns that when AI is left to chance, adoption fragments and risk rises. UNESCO's higher-education survey found that formal institutional policy remains uneven, with many institutions still uncertain or developing frameworks rather than operating settled governance regimes.

5.4 Evaluation, open models, and the case for grounded smaller systems

6. Strategic synthesis: the position your keynote can own
The strongest keynote position sits between two inadequate poles. On one side is institutional boosterism: adopt faster, integrate more deeply, and trust the market to deliver what universities need. On the other side is total refusal: treat generative AI as inherently corrosive and aim mainly to resist it. Your abstract already suggests a more useful third position: engaged stewardship.
The third position in one paragraph
Engaged stewardship means universities neither retreat from AI nor surrender to it. They accept that AI will be part of the operating environment, but they refuse to treat vendor defaults as destiny. They build enough institutional capability to evaluate systems, negotiate terms, protect educational data, preserve exit options, and collaborate across the sector where single institutions are too small to act alone. They may buy some things, build some things, adapt some things, and reject some things—but they do so from a position of deliberate governance rather than dependency by drift.
Why this position is stronger than generic ethics talk
• It turns values into decisions. Ethics alone can remain decorative; procurement, data terms, evaluation, and architectural choices are operational.
• It speaks to the real structure of the ecosystem. Concentrated markets and capital asymmetries mean that institutions need bargaining strategy, not just aspiration.
• It matches the mood of the conference. The room is practical, not theoretical; stewardship gives a constructive answer without sounding naive.
• It keeps students and academic purpose at the centre. Infrastructure is judged by whom it serves and under what conditions.
A possible high-level formulation for the keynote
AI is becoming part of higher education's invisible infrastructure. That fact changes the question universities must ask. The issue is no longer simply whether a given tool is impressive or convenient. The issue is who controls the stack on which teaching, assessment, support, and governance increasingly rely. Because that stack is concentrated, expensive, and often externally owned, universities are now choosing whether to become passive customers in an extractive ecosystem or active stewards of the systems they depend on. The window for making that choice is small because standards, contracts, data arrangements, and institutional norms are hardening now.
A useful definition of control Control does not mean owning every layer. It means being able to understand, evaluate, contest, negotiate, and, when necessary, switch or reconfigure the systems on which core university functions depend.

7. Practical policy agenda for universities
This section translates the argument into institutional moves. The point is not to produce a maximal wish-list, but to define the practical capacities that distinguish a steward from a passive customer. For each area, the keynote can offer a minimum move now, a stronger medium-term move, and a long-term direction.
7.1 Procurement: treat buying decisions as governance decisions
AI procurement is one of the most under-discussed control points in higher education. If procurement is weak, ethics remain rhetorical. Contracts and purchasing criteria determine which models are used, how data flow, what can be audited, which features can be switched off, whether outputs can be exported, and what happens when services change or fail.
Level
What to do
Minimum move now
Use a cross-functional AI procurement checklist involving IT, legal, teaching and learning, data governance, accessibility, procurement, and academic representation.
Stronger move
Require vendors to specify underlying model dependencies, data use for training or improvement, logging and retention practices, evaluation evidence, accessibility features, bias mitigation steps, and continuity arrangements.
Longer-term direction
Develop shared sector procurement standards and bargaining consortia so universities are not negotiating core clauses alone.
Procurement questions worth asking explicitly
• Which model or models power the product, and can that dependency change without customer notice?
• Are user prompts, outputs, metadata, or feedback used to train, fine-tune, or improve either the product itself or any external base model?
• Can the institution opt out of all model-improvement uses of its data?
• What evidence exists that the system performs well on higher-education tasks rather than only on generic benchmarks?
• What independent evaluation has been done, and can the institution run or commission its own tests?
• What logging, explainability, and audit features exist for misconduct reviews, appeals, or quality assurance?
• What export, portability, and off-ramp provisions exist if the institution later wants to switch provider or self-host alternatives?
• What happens during an outage, legal dispute, or upstream model change? What are the service-continuity and fallback arrangements?
• How does the system meet accessibility requirements and support equitable use?
• Who bears liability for harmful outputs, rights infringements, or misleading advice in high-stakes contexts?
7.2 Data governance: treat educational data as governed institutional assets
The data-governance question is not only about compliance. It is about purpose, rights, legitimacy, and institutional trust. Student work, feedback interactions, advising records, and usage traces are not free raw material. Universities need clear distinctions between data used to operate a service, data used to evaluate a service, and data used to improve or train models.
Level
What to do
Minimum move now
Define approved, restricted, and prohibited data categories for AI use. Make it easy for staff and students to know what should never be pasted into public tools.
Stronger move
Create documented pathways for secure local experimentation, de-identification, teacher-generated corpora, and rights-cleared datasets for retrieval or fine-tuning.
Longer-term direction
Build institutional or sectoral governance structures for educational data use in AI, including student representation, purpose limitation, retention rules, and oversight of training uses.
Key data-governance principles to name
• Purpose limitation: only collect and use what is genuinely needed for a clear educational purpose.
• Rights awareness: respect privacy, copyright, consent, and the particular sensitivity of student work and support data.
• Separation of functions: distinguish operational delivery from model training and from research use.
• Transparency: staff and students should know when AI is present, what data it uses, and what institutional position governs that use.
• Proportionality: use the least intrusive method that achieves the educational purpose.
• Oversight: high-impact uses should not be left to local enthusiasm alone.
7.3 Evaluation: make model assessment a core university capability
Evaluation is the quiet centrepiece of the stewardship argument. Without evaluation, universities cannot distinguish fluency from reliability, impressive demos from genuine educational value, or acceptable from unacceptable risk. This applies whether the system is bought from a vendor, accessed via a public API, or hosted locally.
Level
What to do
Minimum move now
Create simple benchmark tasks for the university's highest-priority use cases: assessment support, feedback, advising, search, writing assistance, or triage.
Stronger move
Develop cross-functional evaluation routines including hallucination checks, rights and privacy checks, fairness review, disciplinary review, and user testing with staff and students.
Longer-term direction
Participate in or help build shared sector evaluation suites, red-teaming networks, and open reporting norms so institutions are not each reinventing assurance from scratch.
What to evaluate
• Accuracy and faithfulness on real institutional tasks
• Robustness under prompt variation and adversarial use
• Ability to cite sources, reveal uncertainty, and fail safely
• Disciplinary adequacy rather than only generic language quality
• Fairness and differential error patterns across student groups or use cases
• Privacy and data leakage risk
• User experience: whether the system actually supports learning, judgment, and staff work rather than merely generating text quickly
7.4 Architecture and resilience: preserve legibility, modularity, and exit options
A university does not need to own every layer of its AI stack, but it does need to understand its dependencies. Infrastructure becomes dangerous when the institution cannot map which functions depend on which upstream services, what data cross boundaries, or how to continue operating if a provider changes terms, fails, or disappears.
Level
What to do
Minimum move now
Map AI dependencies in critical workflows: where teaching, assessment, support, or governance functions rely on upstream cloud or model providers.
Stronger move
Prefer modular architectures where possible: retrieval layers, model abstraction layers, and exportable data structures that reduce single-vendor lock-in.
Longer-term direction
Invest selectively in local hosting, open models, or shared services for the highest-value or highest-risk use cases where control and continuity matter most.
Resilience questions
• If the service went down for a day during assessment week, what would fail?
• If the vendor changed its underlying model, how would we know whether quality improved or degraded?
• If a legal or commercial dispute interrupted service, what could we still do locally?
• Could we export our prompts, logs, configuration, and evaluation records in a usable format?
• Would students and staff know where AI was embedded and what the fallback route was?
7.5 Sector-level collaboration: universities are too small to bargain alone
Single institutions often lack bargaining power, evaluation capacity, or compute budgets at the scale the ecosystem now demands. That makes collaboration a practical necessity rather than a nice extra. The keynote can be strong here without sounding utopian: shared procurement clauses, shared evaluation suites, model cards for educational tasks, and collective guidance on data terms are all feasible forms of collaboration.
Level
What to do
Minimum move now
Share procurement questions, evaluation templates, and approved-use guidance across institutions rather than treating them as local secrets.
Stronger move
Create regional or mission-based consortia for model evaluation, secure experimentation, and bargaining with vendors.
Longer-term direction
Develop public-interest AI infrastructure for higher education: shared open models, retrieval services, domain datasets, and governance frameworks with student and staff oversight.
7.6 Pedagogy, work, and student rights: do not separate governance from educational purpose
The keynote should not let policy feel detached from teaching and learning. Infrastructure choices shape the conditions under which students draft, revise, verify, disclose, appeal, and receive support. They shape how staff exercise professional judgment and how workload is redistributed. Stewardship therefore has to include pedagogic design, staff capability, and student rights.

Institutions need

The agenda in one contrast A useful contrast for the talk: the passive customer asks, 'Which tool should we buy?' The steward asks, 'Which capabilities do we need to govern, and which relationships will preserve mission, rights, and resilience?'
A concise Monday-morning agenda
• Map where AI is already embedded in assessment, feedback, support, and admin workflows.
• Create or tighten an AI procurement checklist and make it cross-functional.
• Define what data may and may not be used in external AI tools.

9. Backup Q&A
These are the questions most likely to arise from leaders, practitioners, and sceptics in the room. Each answer is written in a way that can be spoken briefly and then expanded if needed.
Are you saying universities should build everything themselves?
No. The alternative to passive dependence is not building every layer in-house. It is preserving enough understanding, bargaining power, evaluation capacity, and exit options that core educational functions are not wholly governed by external defaults.

Isn't this just anti-vendor politics?
No. Universities will continue to work with vendors. The question is whether those relationships are structured as governable partnerships or as one-way dependencies. Good vendors should welcome clearer standards, stronger evaluation, and better institutional capability.

Can smaller or open models really compete with the frontier?
Not on every generic benchmark. But they do not have to. For many institutional tasks, grounded and well-evaluated smaller systems can be more appropriate, more controllable, and more cost-effective than the largest general models.

Isn't buying from the market simply more realistic than trying to do all this locally?
Buying from the market is realistic. Buying carelessly is not. The point of stewardship is to buy intelligently, know the dependencies, protect data, require evidence, and collaborate where a single institution lacks scale.

Students are already using public tools anyway. Isn't this too late?
It is too late for a denial strategy and too early for resignation. Student use being mainstream is exactly why institutions need better guidance, clearer assessment design, approved options, and more visible governance now.

Won't tighter governance slow innovation?
Bad governance slows innovation by creating fear and inconsistency. Good governance speeds useful innovation because it clarifies what is allowed, what is risky, and what evidence is needed.

Why focus on control when universities are under huge financial pressure?
Because financial pressure can turn short-term convenience into long-term dependency. Control matters most when budgets are tight, because switching costs, opaque pricing, and hidden data terms become even more consequential.

Are you underestimating how fast frontier models are improving?
No. Faster improvement is actually part of the argument. When capabilities move quickly, institutions need more evaluation and stronger architectural discipline, not less, because today's product may rely on tomorrow's unknown upstream changes.

What would one small university realistically do first?
Start with an AI dependency map, a procurement checklist, simple data rules, and one evaluation routine for a high-priority use case. Then look for partners rather than acting alone on everything.

How do we use institutional data ethically for fine-tuning or retrieval?
By being selective and governed: de-identify where possible, separate operational data from training data, obtain the right legal and ethical basis, involve oversight, and avoid treating student work as free raw material.

Is the real problem AI, or the wider platform model of higher education technology?
It is both. AI intensifies a pre-existing platform and cloud dependency problem. That is why the keynote talks about invisible infrastructure rather than about a single tool category.

What should students hear in this argument?
That the issue is not simply whether they use AI. It is whether the systems shaping their education are fair, transparent, governable, and accountable to their interests rather than only to vendor incentives.

How do you measure success if an institution takes stewardship seriously?
Success looks like clearer approved-use pathways, fewer unmanaged risky uses, better evidence on what works, stronger contractual terms, greater resilience, and decisions that remain visibly tied to educational purpose.

Where does academic freedom fit into this?
Academic freedom matters, but it sits inside institutional responsibility. Stewardship should widen meaningful choice and support critical use, not impose a single approved way of thinking or teaching.

A one-minute answer if someone asks, 'So what is your actual proposal?'
My proposal is that universities stop treating AI adoption as mainly a matter of guidance and start treating it as a matter of stewardship. That means buying more carefully, governing educational data more seriously, building evaluation capability, preserving architectural options, and collaborating across the sector where no single institution has enough leverage. The aim is not to own everything. It is to ensure that the systems higher education comes to depend on remain accountable to academic purpose and student interests.

Mini-glossary
Term
Working definition for the keynote
Invisible infrastructure
Systems so embedded in ordinary practice that they disappear from view until they fail, become contested, or shape behaviour in unwanted ways.
Path dependence
The way early choices and reinforcing feedback make some future options easier and others harder.
Stewardship
The active governance of systems so they remain legible, evaluable, negotiable, and aligned with mission.
Grounding
The extent to which an AI system is connected to the relevant world, context, data, and task constraints rather than only generic language patterns.
Evaluation
Structured testing of whether a system works adequately and safely for the tasks and values that matter in context.
Extractivism
The logic of taking value from people, environments, or institutions without reciprocal stewardship or fair return.
Surveillance capitalism
A regime in which human experience and behaviour are captured as data and converted into prediction and influence.
Hyperscaler
A very large cloud and infrastructure provider operating at a scale far beyond ordinary enterprise or public-sector actors.
Lock-in
A condition in which switching away from a system becomes costly because of technical, contractual, social, or organisational dependencies.

A compact closing formula
You can condense the keynote into four sentences if needed. AI is becoming part of higher education's invisible infrastructure. Student adoption and institutional dependence are already mainstream. The infrastructure layer is increasingly shaped by concentrated external actors, so the real question is not merely use but control. The practical alternative to passive dependence is stewardship through procurement, data governance, evaluation, resilience, and sector collaboration.
